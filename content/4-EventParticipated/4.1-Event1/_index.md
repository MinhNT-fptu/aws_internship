## Summary of Content and Lessons Learned

### 1. The Iceberg of Procrastination: When “Laziness” Is Just the Tip of Fear

#### Speaker Information

The speaker of the session was **Khắc Uy Pham**, a third-year student at Vietnamese-German University.

The presentation focused on procrastination in studying, work, and personal life.

#### Main Content

The session explained that procrastination is not simply a form of “laziness.” According to the speaker, laziness is only the visible tip of the iceberg, while the hidden part underneath often consists of fears such as:

- Fear of not being good enough.
- Fear of being judged by others.
- Fear of making mistakes.
- Fear of failure.

The speaker also analyzed the negative cycle of procrastination. When facing a difficult task, people often avoid it by scrolling through social media, playing games, or doing other activities to experience temporary comfort.

However, feelings of guilt, stress, and self-blame increase afterward. This makes it even more difficult to begin the original task and creates a continuous cycle of procrastination.

In addition to analyzing its causes, the presentation introduced several methods for overcoming procrastination, including:

- Identifying and naming personal fears accurately.
- Applying the five-minute rule.
- Breaking work down into smaller and simpler tasks.
- Starting with very small actions.
- Not waiting until one feels confident enough to take action.

The speaker emphasized that action should come before confidence. Instead of waiting until they feel completely ready, people should actively take the first step to build momentum and continue moving forward.

#### Lessons Learned

The most important lesson from the session was that procrastination should be understood according to its true nature. Instead of blaming themselves for being lazy, people should ask themselves:

> “What am I afraid of?”

Once people identify their fears, they can more easily find suitable ways to overcome them.

I also realized that we should not wait for the “perfect moment” to begin. Breaking work into smaller parts and completing the first step, even if it is very small, can create motivation to continue completing the remaining tasks.

In studying and working, especially in the technology field, the **“Go build”** mindset is very important: start, experiment, fail fast, learn quickly, and continue improving.

### 2. Why We Always Need Confidence

#### Speaker Information

The speaker of the session was **Nguyễn Thị Quỳnh Như**.

The presentation focused on the role of confidence in studying, student life, professional development, and social relationships.

#### Main Content

The presentation began by clarifying the meaning of confidence.

According to the speaker, confidence does not mean:

- Having an excessively large ego.
- Always being perfect.
- Knowing everything.
- Always having all the answers.

Confidence is the ability to try, take action, and express oneself even when feeling worried or uncertain.

The speaker also analyzed the negative effects of lacking confidence in student life. Self-doubt can cause people to:

- Miss valuable opportunities.
- Fail to demonstrate their true abilities.
- Avoid presenting their ideas.
- Avoid speaking in front of others.
- Create invisible pressure for themselves.

Many people possess strong knowledge and skills but are afraid to demonstrate them, defend their ideas, or actively take advantage of opportunities.

Another important part of the presentation focused on the psychological causes of low confidence, including:

- Impostor syndrome.
- The Dunning–Kruger effect.
- Fear of being judged.
- Frequently comparing oneself with others.

The speaker emphasized that professional skills are the foundation, but confidence is the bridge that helps people communicate, collaborate, defend their ideas, and take responsibility for their decisions.

The speaker also suggested several ways to build confidence in daily life, such as:

- Preparing carefully before presentations, interviews, or projects.
- Recognizing small achievements and personal progress.
- Actively participating in group activities.
- Applying the five-second rule to overcome hesitation.
- Not being afraid of making mistakes and learning from them.

#### Lessons Learned

The lesson I learned was that confidence is not entirely an inborn quality. It is a skill that can be built and developed over time.

To become more confident, people should begin with small actions, prepare more carefully, and recognize their own progress.

The session also helped me realize that professional knowledge alone is not enough. In studying and working environments, confidence helps us:

- Present ideas more clearly.
- Communicate more effectively.
- Build better connections with others.
- Actively take advantage of opportunities.
- Develop personally and professionally.

Therefore, confidence is one of the most important factors that enables people to fully demonstrate and develop their abilities.

### 3. The Hidden Iceberg of a Project: DevOps Before Disaster

#### Speaker Information

The speaker of the session was **Trần Minh Quân**.

The presentation focused on the hidden problems beneath a software project and the role of DevOps in preventing risks before incidents occur.

#### Main Content

The presentation used the iceberg model to explain common problems in software projects.

The visible part of the iceberg represents symptoms that can be easily observed, such as:

- Missing project deadlines.
- Errors occurring in the production environment.
- Failed deployments.
- Customer complaints.
- Overworked and exhausted team members.

However, the hidden part beneath the surface contains the actual causes, including:

- Unclear project requirements.
- Lack of communication among team members.
- Teams working in isolation.
- Lack of ownership and responsibility.
- Manual working processes.
- Slow feedback loops.
- Lack of coordination between development and operations teams.

The speaker emphasized that addressing only the visible symptoms is not enough. If a team only reacts when errors occur, continuously “puts out fires,” and fixes surface-level problems, similar issues will continue to happen.

Instead, teams should apply systems thinking to identify and address the root causes of problems.

The main focus of the presentation was the fundamental mindset of DevOps. DevOps is not limited to tools such as:

- Docker.
- Kubernetes.
- CI/CD.
- Cloud.
- Terraform.

DevOps is a combination of three important elements:

1. **People**
2. **Processes**
3. **Technology**

The speaker explained that deployment processes can be automated, but trust, communication, and ownership among team members cannot be automated.

The presentation also introduced several important DevOps principles, including:

- Strengthening collaboration to remove barriers between teams.
- Using automation to reduce manual errors.
- Building fast feedback loops to detect problems early.
- Continuously improving to prevent repeated mistakes.
- Sharing responsibility throughout the entire product lifecycle.

#### Lessons Learned

The most important lesson I learned was that the visible problems in a project are often only the results of deeper underlying causes.

For a project to operate effectively, the team needs to actively identify and address root causes instead of only fixing errors after incidents have already occurred.

The session also helped me understand that DevOps is not only for technical engineers. It is also related to how the entire team works together.

Clear communication, shared responsibility, process automation, and fast feedback are important factors that help projects reduce major risks.

For people studying or working in the technology field, this is an important lesson in building a sustainable, efficient, and continuously improving workflow.

### 4. Level Up Your AWS Skills with Cloud Quest and Floci

#### Speaker Information

The speaker of the session was **Huỳnh Thái Linh**.

The presentation focused on methods for improving AWS skills through AWS Cloud Quest and the open-source tool Floci.

#### Main Content

The presentation began by discussing common difficulties faced by beginners who are learning AWS through practical exercises.

Many learners commonly experience problems such as:

- Worrying about the cost of using AWS services.
- Frequently checking their bills.
- Being afraid of forgetting to delete created resources.
- Being concerned about unexpected charges.
- Not knowing which service to begin learning first.

To address these problems, the speaker introduced **AWS Cloud Quest** as a free and beginner-friendly practical learning environment.

AWS Cloud Quest allows learners to explore AWS through game-based missions. This helps them understand AWS architecture and services in a more visual and engaging way.

The speaker then introduced **Floci**, an open-source tool that supports testing AWS services in a local environment.

Floci works as an AWS service emulator, helping learners and developers to:

- Test architectures more quickly.
- Write and test code on their personal computers.
- Reduce cloud usage costs.
- Avoid unexpected charges.
- Shorten development and testing time.

The presentation also compared Floci with LocalStack based on several criteria, including:

- Speed.
- Performance.
- Storage usage.
- AWS service support.
- Cost.

However, the speaker also pointed out several limitations of Floci:

- It does not fully support every AWS service.
- Some services only return simulated results.
- Results in a local environment may differ from those in an actual cloud environment.
- It cannot completely replace testing on real AWS infrastructure.

Finally, the speaker proposed a three-stage AWS learning path:

1. **Stage 1:** Use AWS Cloud Quest to understand cloud architecture and fundamental concepts.
2. **Stage 2:** Use Floci to write code and perform quick tests in a local environment.
3. **Stage 3:** Deploy applications on real AWS infrastructure to test them in an actual environment.

#### Lessons Learned

The important lesson from the session was that learning AWS requires an appropriate learning path to reduce pressure on beginners.

Instead of immediately deploying applications to the cloud and constantly worrying about costs, learners can begin with simulated environments or game-based learning methods to build a strong foundation.

In addition, learners should know how to combine different tools throughout the learning process:

- AWS Cloud Quest helps develop architectural thinking.
- Floci supports fast and cost-effective testing.
- Real AWS infrastructure helps learners gain experience with actual deployment environments.

However, learners should also understand the limitations of each tool and avoid becoming completely dependent on simulated environments.
